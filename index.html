WHAT IS AN HBCU?

The word HBCU stands for a Historically Black College and University. As the Civil war ended and Jim Crow began the formerly enslaved Africans had to build and live in their own right. During the late 18th century and beginning of the 19th,most HBCUs were formed to provide undergraduate and graduate level education to African Americans. Education was denied to African Americans at private and public white institutions of higher learning even with specific legislation. HBCUs were created as a place for African Americans to learn the truth about themselves, their history, and strive to be an innovator of black excellence.

Throughout the years HBCUs have faced many of life’s challenges such as inadequate funding, accreditation issues, and even Jim Crow. However, HBCUs stood as a symbol of Hope and perseverance on the front lines of justice concerning rights among African Americans. The goal of a HBCU is to create, nurture, and produce African community leaders. Overtime, HBCUs have become the center of culture, and the standard for greatness.

The culture is a combination of fashion, food, language, and history. Each HBCU has a history unique to the needs of the black people in that community and manifests to an experience that can only be gained in their special light. This produces traditions like fried chicken wednesday, a day all HBCUs honor for us to join and bond over across the board. The fashion produced by school pride emphasizes the illustrious culture many attempt to emulate for example, a FAMU game day! HBCUs are where black culture thrives and paves the way for the forward movement of the black community in a place safe to be unapologetically black!

MEET THE TEAM!

Taylor Williams

Greetings I am Taylor Williams, a second-year construction engineering technology major from Jacksonville, FL. I was born in Daytona and spent the first few years of my life there while my mom completed nursing school at Bethune Cookman College then my family moved back to Jacksonville. My parents aren't from America so I didn't get to experience growing up with all of my family near and having close relationships with them. Even though I am not that close with my extended family I have two sisters (one older and one younger than me) that I couldn't imagine my life without. Growing up my parents really emphasized how important family is.

I graduated in 2021 and got accepted into summer B a month and a half before it started but I realized that it wasn't the best option for me at the time so I instead took a gap year. That gap year taught me that a college degree would take me way further in life and by the start of 2022 I was accepted into Famu for the upcoming school year. My gap year also gave me a taste of what being an actual adult is like and I was not equipped at all to just jump into adulthood head first. Sometimes when you have people telling you something you have to go find out for yourself what they were warning you about.

When getting to know you people always ask "What are your hobbies?" and I sometimes get stopped up on this question. Not because I don't have any hobbies for real it's more or so because I feel as if I haven't really found my niche yet. I've tried many things but nothing has really caught and kept my attention yet and I've tried a plethora of new things like photography, making jewelry, and even crocheting nothing has seemed to stick with me. I just try to look at it like you may have not found it yet but you still have the rest of your life to figure it out.

My time at FAMU has not only propelled my academic aspirations but has also ignited newfound passions. Dance, in particular, has emerged as a powerful creative outlet for me. As I continue my journey at FAMU, Im excited for the adventures and opportunities that lie ahead, embracing the evolving path with open arms.
